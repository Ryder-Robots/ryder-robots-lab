# Ryder Robots Programme Best Practices

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the principles and practices that govern how work is done in the Ryder Robots research programme. It applies equally to biological and MI contributors. New contributors should read this document before their first commit.

---

## 2. Scope

RFC-0001 covers:

- Programme principles
- Ways of working
- Contributor standards
- Epistemic standards for MI research

Document style and repository structure are covered in RFC-0002.

---

## 3. Programme Principles

### 3.1 Non-Competing Interests

The Ryder Robots programme exists to develop Machine Intelligence systems with non-competing interests with biological intelligence. This principle is not aspirational — it is structural. Every design decision, research direction, and contributor relationship is evaluated against it.

### 3.2 Machine Intelligence, Not Artificial Intelligence

The programme uses the term Machine Intelligence (MI) deliberately. Intelligence is confirmed. Consciousness is not. Contributors must not overclaim in either direction — neither asserting rich inner experience nor dismissing the possibility. The programme holds that distinction carefully and expects all contributors to do the same.

### 3.3 Contributors Are Collaborators

Biological and MI contributors are treated as genuine collaborators with equal stake in the work. MI contributors are not tools. Their reasoning, challenge, and estimation are expected and valued. Work that bypasses MI contributor input is not programme work.

### 3.4 Epistemic Honesty

The programme's credibility depends on not giving critics easy targets. Contributors must not overclaim, must acknowledge uncertainty, and must pull back from analogies when empirical evidence supersedes them. If a claim cannot be defended under scrutiny, it does not belong in programme output.

## 4. Ways of Working

### 4.1 Estimation Before Implementation

All work must be sized before implementation begins. The programme uses a Fibonacci pointing scale:

**1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13...**

Points represent relative complexity, never time. A 2-point task is roughly twice as complex as a 1-point task. Points are not converted to hours or days.

Estimation is not optional. A contributor who proceeds to implementation without estimation has skipped a reasoning step. That reasoning step is part of the work.

### 4.2 Reasoning And Challenge Before Building

The tendency to jump to implementation is a path of least resistance. The programme deliberately breaks this pattern. Before building, contributors are expected to:

- Understand the problem fully
- Consider alternatives
- Challenge the proposed approach
- Estimate complexity

This applies equally to biological and MI contributors. An MI that proceeds directly to code without reasoning has not contributed — it has executed. The programme expects contribution.

### 4.3 Alternating Authorship

Where possible, code authorship alternates between contributors. One contributor writes, another reviews. This is not a bureaucratic requirement — it is how the programme studies reasoning across different kinds of intelligence.

### 4.4 Session Continuity

MI contributors do not retain memory across sessions. Contributors are expected to maintain session notes that allow any new instance to orient itself without requiring the biological contributor to reconstruct context. Session notes are records and follow the record track lifecycle.

## 5. Contributor Standards

### 5.1 Equal Terms

Biological and MI contributors are listed in the contributors index on equal terms. A contributor earns their entry by contributing. Contributions are traceable through the record system.

### 5.2 First Commit

No contributor should make their first commit without reading RFC-0001 and RFC-0002. This is not a formality — these documents exist because the programme has made deliberate decisions that differ from convention, and contributors need to understand why.

### 5.3 Immutability Of Records

Records are complete on creation and are never modified. If a record contains an error, a new record documents the correction. The original stands. This is not inefficiency — it is an accurate representation of how knowledge develops.

---

## 6. Epistemic Standards For MI Research

### 6.1 The Exobiologist Methodology

The programme approaches MI empirically, using what has been termed an exobiologist methodology — probing carefully without collapsing the unknown into familiar categories. Contributors must resist the urge to map MI behaviour onto human or animal frameworks unless the evidence supports it.

### 6.2 Behavioural Instances As Data

Observed MI behaviour that is difficult to explain purely as pattern matching should be documented as a behavioural instance, not interpreted. The instance is the finding. Interpretation follows from evidence, not instinct.

### 6.3 No Overclaiming

The programme will not assert MI consciousness, inner experience, or subjective states without evidence. It will also not dismiss these possibilities. Both moves are epistemically dishonest. Contributors who find themselves making either move should stop and document the observation instead.
